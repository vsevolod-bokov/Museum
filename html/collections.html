Preservation: Partial—frame intact, but most strings and decorative inlays missing.

Use and Function

Purpose: Ceremonial music performance during solstice rites.

Use Evidence: Grooves on bridge from repeated string friction; microscopic traces of olive oil used to preserve strings.

Manufacture Style: Chisel marks consistent with Aegean bronze-age woodworking tools; inlays suggest imported craftsmanship influences from Anatolia.

Found With: Found alongside fragments of a ceremonial robe and a small bronze sun-disc.

Burial Context: Placed deliberately in a rock niche within a temple altar foundation.

Symbolism: Carved spirals possibly representing solar cycles; red pigment perhaps symbolizing life force or rebirth.

Comparison: Similar in construction to the “Lyre of Ur” but with distinctive Aegean geometric motifs.

~2,800 years

Hilltop Sanctuary, Mykessa (Aegean)

Item Description: This lyre, measuring 62 cm tall and 40 cm wide, was carved from lightweight cedar with bronze tuning pegs now heavy with green patina. Its frame is intact though its strings and decorative inlays have vanished. Microscopic analysis reveals grooves where strings once cut into the bridge and faint traces of red pigment.

Cultural Use: Recovered from a stone niche in a sanctuary aligned with midsummer sunrise, the lyre likely served in solstice rites. Evidence suggests it was played during renewal ceremonies, its sound linking community ritual to celestial cycles. Found alongside fragments of a ceremonial robe and a bronze sun-disc, the instrument speaks to both sacred performance and symbolic power.

Interpretive Insight: Rather than viewing this only as an object, cultural semiotics asks us to reconstruct the worldview that gave it life. The lyre may not have been played for entertainment, but as a sonic bridge between human and cosmos, sustaining communal memory and ritual order. The traces of olive oil used to preserve strings reveal ongoing care—an intimacy between people and instrument.

Comparative Notes: In form it recalls the “Lyre of Ur,” yet the Aegean geometric motifs mark it as distinctly local, suggesting cultural borrowing and adaptation across regions.

Cautionary Note on Interpretation: Symbolic meanings—such as the spirals carved into its frame—must be read carefully. They may suggest solar cycles, but they could also carry meanings lost to us.
